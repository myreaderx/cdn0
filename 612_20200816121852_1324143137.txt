What does &#8220;with ease&#8221; mean though? For us it means without causing exceptions (either customer-facing or internally) or job failures.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>For HEY, we are able to get away with running the entire front-end stack (<a href=\"https://openresty.org/en/\">OpenResty</a>, <a href=\"https://github.com/puma/puma\">Puma</a>) and the bulk of our async background jobs (<a href=\"https://github.com/resque/resque\">Resque</a>) on spot.</p>\n\n\n\n<h3>Enduring workloads</h3>\n\n\n\n<p>What doesn&#8217;t fit well on spot instances? For HEY, we&#8217;ve found three main categories: long-running jobs, Redis instances for <a href=\"https://m.signalvnoise.com/seamless-branch-deploys-with-kubernetes/\">branch deploys</a>, and anything requiring a <a href=\"https://kubernetes.io/docs/concepts/storage/persistent-volumes/\">persistent volume</a> (like certain pieces of the mail pipeline). It runs as a DaemonSet and continuously watches the EC2 metadata service to see if the current node has been issued any spot termination notifications (or scheduled maintenance notifications). If one is found, it&#8217;ll (attempt to) gracefully remove the running pods so that Kubernetes can schedule them elsewhere with no user impact.</p>\n\n\n\n<h3> cluster-autoscaler</h3>\n\n\n\n<p><a href=\"https://github.com/kubernetes/autoscaler/tree/master/cluster-autoscaler\">cluster-autoscaler</a> doesn&#8217;t need many changes to run with a spot auto-scaling group. Since we&#8217;re talking about ensuring that instance termination behaviors are handled appropriately though, you should know about the pain we went through to get ASG rebalancing handled properly. When rebalancing, we&#8217;d see nodes that just died and the ALB would continue to send traffic to them via kube-proxy because there was no warning to kubelet that they were about to go away. aws-node-termination-handler <a href=\"https://github.com/aws/aws-node-termination-handler/issues/95\">cannot handle rebalancing</a> currently, but I believe that <a href=\"https://docs.aws.amazon.com/eks/latest/userguide/managed-node-groups.html\">EKS managed nodegroups</a> do &#8212; at the expense of not supporting spot instances. Of course there are other obligations for running in other regions &#8212; are all of the services you use supported, can you reach other infrastructure you may have, etc. (and it&#8217;s also possible that moving to another region for price alone just doesn&#8217;t make sense for you, that&#8217;s totally fine!).</p>\n\n\n\n<h2>Leveling up</h2>\n\n\n\n<p>Merely running compute on spot isn&#8217;t the end-goal though, and there are several paths that we can take to continue to level-up our compute infrastructure to place resources optimally, both from a cost standpoint and for keeping things close to the end user.</p>\n\n\n\n<ul><li>[shorter term] <strong>Using the <a href=\"https://kubernetes.io/docs/tasks/run-application/horizontal-pod-autoscale/\">Horizontal Pod Autoscaler</a> to automatically scale <a href=\"https://kubernetes.io/docs/concepts/workloads/controllers/deployment/\">deployment</a> replica counts as traffic to the platform regularly ebbs and flows.</strong> When combined with cluster-autoscaler, Kubernetes (with a lot of trust from your ops team&#8230;..) can take care of scaling your infrastructure automatically based on CPU utilization, request latency, request count, etc. HEY is already starting to show a standard traffic pattern where usage is highest during the US work hours and then drops greatly overnight and on the weekends. These are perfect times to scale the app down to a smaller deployment size. Front-end compute isn&#8217;t the biggest cost driver for HEY (that falls to persistent data storage infrastructure like Aurora and Elasticache), but scaling <em>something</em> down when you can is better than not scaling anything at all.</li><li>[long term] <strong>Shunting compute between regions and AZs based on spot pricing.</strong> This is likely to not be something that a company the size of Basecamp ever needs to think about, but being able to shunt traffic around to different regions and AZs based on compute cost and availability is a dream stretch goal. Text on a page, maybe a masthead graphic of some sort. Nothing sophisticated. It often looked like traditional letterhead, or a printed newsletter, but now on the screen. Interactions were few, if any, but perhaps a couple links tied a nascent site together.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>And there was the other extreme. At that time, Web design wasn&#8217;t web design &#8211; it was print design, multimedia/interactive design, and graphic design. The same is true for the difference between in-office work and remote work. Yup, it&#8217;s all still the same work, but it&#8217;s a different <em>way</em> to work.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>In-office and remote work are different <em>platforms of work</em>. It&#8217;s a way, but it&#8217;s the wrong way.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Simulating in-person office work remotely does both approaches a disservice.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>This is often what happens when change is abrupt. We bring what we know from one to the other. We apply what we&#8217;re familiar with to the unfamiliar. But, in time, we recognize that doesn&#8217;t work.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>The enlightened companies coming out of this pandemic will be the ones that figured out <a href=\"https://basecamp.com/books/remote\">the right way to work remotely</a>. They&#8217;ll have stopped trying to make remote look like local. They won&#8217;t be the ones that can&#8217;t wait to pull everyone back to the office, they&#8217;ll be the ones that spot the advantages of optionality, and recognize a wonderful resilience in being able to work from anywhere.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>And they&#8217;ll be the ones that finally realize that there&#8217;s nothing magical about the office. Prototype</figcaption></figure>\n\n\n\n<p>Having explored lots of different examples and core ideas (including the nascent version of <a href=\"https://hey.com/features/the-screener/\">The Screener</a>, the <a href=\"https://hey.com/features/all-files/\">All Files view</a>, and contact pages), we felt like we had something promising. </p>\n\n\n\n<p>None of it really worked, there were still a million unknowns, and loads of features we hadn&#8217;t figured out yet, but there was one thing we were sure about‚Äîit had evolved far beyond just a better Highrise. If we were going to build it, it needed to become a complete email service. And if it was going to be an email service, it needed to have a personal version, of course!</p>\n\n\n\n<hr class=\"wp-block-separator\"/>\n\n\n\n<p><strong>Phase 2: Building it</strong></p>\n\n\n\n<p>By early 2019 we started the process of making HEY real. At first we used the prototype&#8217;s general skeleton as a foundation to start wiring things up, but we continued to hone the app&#8217;s visual design. Our brilliant marketing designer <a aria-label=\"undefined (opens in a new tab)\" href=\"https://twitter.com/AdamStddrd\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noreferrer noopener\">Adam</a> joined up, and he helped us shed all the experimental cruft from the original design, paring things back and making the whole thing more minimal.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Adam also developed the original color palettes and menu gradients that became iconic‚Äîthose bright pops of color are sprinkled throughout and give the app a lot of friendly personality without being too overwhelming.</p>\n\n\n\n<figure a team where everyone has autonomy is a bit like playing in a band. Someone seeds an idea, and then someone else builds on it and takes it somewhere else, then those changes get promoted into little systems, and over time the best ideas persist through all the changes. Harmonies prevail. </p>\n\n\n\n<p>You can see that taking place in the screenshots above, but it really picked up steam from mid-2019 on, when the whole the company started working in earnest. For a while there was a lot of tug-of-war design and development between the web and mobile teams, where all of us bent and stretched the system for what we needed on our respective platforms. Since HEY is a hybrid web app, we ended up building a ton of new tech to optimize for UI patterns that would work equally well on a phone or a laptop (we&#8217;ll be sharing a lot more about that soon.) <a aria-label=\"undefined (opens in a new tab)\" href=\"https://twitter.com/conormuirhead\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noreferrer noopener\">Conor</a> led the way on a lot of those efforts.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>By early 2020 we had worked out most of the fundamentals.
