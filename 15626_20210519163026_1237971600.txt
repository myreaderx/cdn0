There, I said it.</p><p>I first heard about SQL in 1997. I was in high school, and as part of a computing class we were working with databases in Microsoft Access. The computers we used were outdated, and the class was boring. Even then, it seemed that SQL was ancient.</p><p>SQL dates back almost 50 years to 1970 when Edgar Codd, a computer scientist working for IBM, wrote&#160;<a class=\"tve-froala\" href=\"https://www.seas.upenn.edu/~zives/03f/cis550/codd.pdf\" style=\"outline: none;\">a paper describing a new system for organizing data in databases</a>. By the end of the decade, several prototypes of Codd’s system had been built, and a query language — the Structured Query Language (SQL) — was born to interact with these databases.</p><p>In the years since, it has been widely adopted. Learning SQL — which can be pronounced either “sequel” or “S.Q.L.”, by the way — has been a rite of passage for programmers who need to work with databases for decades.</p><p><strong>But why should someone who wants to get a job in data spend time learning this ‘ancient’ language in 2021?</strong></p><p>Why not spend all your time mastering Python/R, or focusing on ‘sexier’ data skills, like Deep Learning, Scala, and Spark?</p></div><div class=\"thrv_wrapper thrv_text_element\"><p>While knowing the fundamentals of a more general-purpose language like Python or R is critical, <strong>ignoring SQL will make it much harder to get a job in data</strong>. SQL may be old, but it’s ubiquitous.</p><p>Data Scientist and former Dataquest student&#160;<a href=\"https://www.dataquest.io/blog/vicknesh-mano/\">Vicknesh</a>&#160;got his first job as a Data Analyst.
