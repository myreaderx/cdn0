The braided vessels are the result of a lengthy, holistic process that extends from foraging the wild fibers to twisting the processed cords into durable little containers. Whether striped, checkered, or coiled in rows, each basket is a testament to Grieve&#8217;s patience and ability to adapt a traditional craft into an unusually tiny form.</p>\n<p>Attuned to the natural rhythms of the region, Grieve harvests materials from the woodlands and fields near her home in the Lake District, U.K., with a focus on the weeds and invasive species that are often regarded as nuisances. &#8220;One of the things I enjoy most about working with wild foraged materials is the awareness it gives you of the seasons and cycles of the plants and the land,&#8221; she says. &#8220;In spring, I gather willow bark and dandelions, in summer nettles and brambles. Autumn is a mad rush of harvesting long leafy things, and in the winter, I spend what little sunlight there is foraging vines such as honeysuckle and ivy.&#8221;</p>\n<p>&#160;</p>\n<p><img loading=\"lazy\" class=\"alignnone size-full wp-image-147150\" src=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/grieve-9.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"2000\" height=\"1814\" srcset=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/grieve-9.jpg 2000w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/grieve-9-640x580.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/grieve-9-960x871.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/grieve-9-1536x1393.jpg 1536w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/grieve-9-624x566.jpg 624w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/grieve-9-640x580@2x.jpg 1280w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/grieve-9-960x871@2x.jpg 1920w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 2000px) 100vw, 2000px\" /></p>\n<p>The plants undergo a painstaking process that involves splitting the stalk, peeling out the soft and spongy pith, drying the remaining fibers, and later rehydrating the strands, a method Grieve developed while working in central France where she was tasked with lining vegetable garden with hazel. &#8220;I felt an immediate connection to the craft, the simple meditative rhythm of the weaving, the beautiful tactile way in which it allows you to connect with the land, and the feeling of self-reliance,&#8221; she says. Today, her focus is on the most abundant and hearty species, which she twists into long cords to create wide, sloping bowls, handled baskets, or pouches just big enough to fit a pebble.</p>\n<p>In addition to creating more goods to sell in <a href=\"https://www.foragedfibres.co.uk/shop\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">her shop</a>, Grieve is currently working on a book detailing her techniques. Nestled into the lush hillside of <a href=\"https://stormking.org/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Storm King Arts Center</a> in New York&#8217;s Hudson Valley, Sze&#8217;s &#8220;Fallen Sky&#8221; is comprised of 132 distinct pieces of polished stainless steel arranged in a fragmented circle.</p>\n<p>The sloping, slightly hidden installation, which is now part of the center&#8217;s permanent collection, reflects the landscape, shifting the mirrored images it displays depending on the time of day, season, and the location of the viewer. All of the grasses surrounding the metal components were chosen and planted by hand, creating a contrast between the sleek tops of the steel and the natural growths.</p>\n<p>Spanning 26 feet, the amorphous, segmented forms evoke the process of erosion and the ways elements change and deteriorate over time. Sze pairs &#8220;Fallen Sky&#8221; with an immersive collaged installation titled &#8220;<a href=\"https://collections.stormking.org/Detail/occurrences/153\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Fifth Season</a>,&#8221; which will be on view inside the center through November 8, an accompaniment that speaks to her vision for the pieces. “The relationship of the human to landscape is this age-old exploration of artists, but both works I’ve made are much more about how the landscape is fragile, it’s in flux, and our relationship to it is fractured,&#8221; she said in <a href=\"https://www.nytimes.com/2021/05/20/arts/sarah-sze-storm-king.html\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">a recent interview</a>. &#8220;I think this has to do with our generation. Her mixed-media collages layer textured graphite gradients and mesh-like cuttings into splintered depictions of her subjects. &#8220;&#8216;Fragmentary&#8217; is one word that I return to again and again because I think portraiture is an act of catching glimmers of a person,&#8221; she tells Colossal. &#8220;I like the idea of not being able to see everything. Having multiple layers partially conceals but the patterns of foliage, (which) also act like a kind of shelter.&#8221;</p>\n<p>For each work, Kim first illustrates a single figure—the subjects shown here are models Yuka Mannami and Hoyeon Jung—and then digitally draws a corresponding botanical pattern. Those motifs are cut with the help of a Silhouette Cameo machine before they&#8217;re built up sheet by sheet. Graceful and at times surreal, the resulting portraits portray fractions of faces and hands that are duplicated or filtered through colorful webs.</p>\n<p>You can dive into Kim&#8217;s process in <a href=\"https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DTUVfGDEAqE&#38;feature=youtu.be\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">this studio visit</a>, and find a larger collection of her tiered illustrations on <a href=\"https://www.instagram.com/christinejhkim/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Instagram</a>. The bony subject finds itself in a variety of bizarre situations, whether bursting from a tube of paint, orchestrating a puppet show with a pair of ornate paper hands, or nervously awaiting an encroaching fire. Often set against backdrops filled with multi-colored dabs of paint, his small pieces are imbued with a sense of creative problem-solving as he traps figures in scenes with boxes of pencils, scuffed erasers, and other craft supplies.</p>\n<p>Although skeletons are typically tied to ideas of death and afterlife, Limon sees the anatomical subject as a universal image that allows him &#8220;to portray a thought, feeling or idea without the identity of the figure getting in the way,&#8221; although he tends to pair the ubiquitous form with actions and environments that are tied to his personal life. &#8220;My biggest concern has always been what’s been going on around closest to me and that is my family. Things were not so easy growing up in our family, and these days we’ve been able to get closer and help each other out,&#8221; he shares.</p>\n<p>Originals, prints, and a few wearables are available in <a href=\"https://jason-limon-art.myshopify.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Limon&#8217;s shop</a>, and you have a few chances to see his unearthly works in the coming months: at the <a href=\"https://www.laartshow.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">LA Art Show</a> from July 29 to August 1, at <a href=\"https://www.copronason.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Copro Gallery</a> in Santa Monica in September and October, and at Long Island&#8217;s <a href=\"https://havengallery.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Haven Gallery</a> in April 2022.
