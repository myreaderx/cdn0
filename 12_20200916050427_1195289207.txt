href=\"https://www.behance.net/gallery/97950301/The-Creative-Specimens?mv=email\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\"><em>The Creative Specimens</em></a> seamlessly combines species into unusual hybrids. Similar in color, each organism is bizarre in form. The feathered head of a bird is placed on a tortoise&#8217;s body, octopus tentacles sprout from the bottom of a cactus, and speckled coral comprises a deer&#8217;s antlers.</p>\n<p><a href=\"https://conference.99u.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Adobe&#8217;s 99U Conference</a> spurred the collaborative project as a way to offer a visual language encompassing various creative careers and passions. Inspired by the biological classifications of Charles Darwin and his contemporaries, New York-based art director and graphic designer <a href=\"https://www.markbrooksdesign.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Mark Brooks</a> digitally rendered the organisms by referencing vintage illustrations. He then passed the project to <a href=\"http://alademosca.com/novaweb/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Joanmiquel Bennasar</a>, an illustrator living and working in the Balearic Islands, who recreated the creatures in watercolor.</p>\n<p>Explore more of <a href=\"https://www.behance.net/markbrooks\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Brooks</a>’s and <a She&#8217;s known for <a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2019/10/kathleen-ryan-moldy-fruit/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">her fruit pieces</a> that appear to be covered in mold, whether in the form of a deflated bunch of grapes or a pair of cherries spotted with fungi.</p>\n<p>Ryan portrays the moldy substances through precious and semi-precious gemstones like amethyst, quartz, and marble. The materials&#8217; durability and longevity directly contrast the decay they represent. Whereas the most valuable and lustrous stones cover parts of the fruit, Ryan uses simple glass beads to create the still supple portions, forming the bright red flesh of the cherry or the pockets of yellow rind on the lemon.</p>\n<p>A virtual exhibition of the artist&#8217;s rotting sculptures, which sometimes span as many as 90 inches wide, is available for viewing from <a href=\"https://karmakarma.org/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Karma</a>. His latest project titled &#8220;<a href=\"https://chrisfox.com.au/projects/interchange-pavilion\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Interchange Pavilion</a>&#8221; similarly repurposes vintage railway tracks to construct a 350-square-meter outdoor pavilion. The work is comprised of 250 meters of stainless-steel rails, 15 tons of glass-reinforced concrete, and 1,400 pieces of hardwood. Built in sections, the rails in &#8220;Interchange Pavilion&#8221; offer several paths upward, where they converge at a central point.</p>\n<p>If you&#8217;re in Sydney, check out the newly-opened public artwork, which was unveiled August 25. A similarly sized object, however, has been upending that saying for 125 years. From political campaigns to punch lines to keepsakes, the button has packed bits of incredibly rich history into just a few inches. &#8220;It seems like a niche little object, but it really tells a very general American history,&#8221; collector and manufacturer <a href=\"https://www.instagram.com/busychristen/?hl=en\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Christen Carter</a> tells Colossal. The wearable item is, in fact, an entry point into the complexities of the past.</p>\n<p>Carter recently co-authored the forthcoming book <em><a href=\"https://bookshop.org/a/96/9781616898700\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Button Power</a></em>—which is available for pre-order on <a href=\"https://bookshop.org/a/96/9781616898700\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Bookshop</a>—with notable dealer <a href=\"https://www.hakes.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Ted Hake</a>, who&#8217;s been collecting the objects for around 60 years. Through composed displays and black-and-white photos, the tome delves into the item&#8217;s history, spanning its invention in 1896 to contemporary usages. &#8220;Early on people were wearing buttons, and mostly it&#8217;s a temporary thing. It&#8217;s a moment in time,&#8221; Carter says. &#8220;They connected you to something else. One-hundred-twenty-five years ago, images weren&#8217;t as prevalent as they are now.&#8221; <em>Button Power </em>compiles a diverse array of notable figures, from Shirley Chisholm and the Ramones to Rube Goldberg and Muhammad Ali, each represented through the wearable item.</p>\n<p>&#160;</p>\n<div id=\"attachment_134737\" style=\"width: 1210px\" class=\"wp-caption alignnone\"><img aria-describedby=\"caption-attachment-134737\" loading=\"lazy\" class=\"wp-image-134737 size-full\" src=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-3.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"1200\" height=\"1350\" srcset=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-3.jpg 1200w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-3-640x720.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-3-960x1080.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-3-768x864.jpg 768w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-3-624x702.jpg 624w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 1200px) 100vw, 1200px\" /><p id=\"caption-attachment-134737\" class=\"wp-caption-text\">From the Obama Inauguration 2012</p></div>\n<p>Originally a casual collector, Carter now is responsible for the world&#8217;s only museum dedicated to the medium, which is housed in the Chicago-based manufacturer <a href=\"https://www.busybeaver.net/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Busy Beaver Button Co</a>. The institution currently boasts more than 40,000 buttons and is accepting donations. Currently, it&#8217;s closed because of COVID-19, although a virtual archive of about 9,000 is available to scroll through on <a href=\"https://www.buttonmuseum.org/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">its site</a>.</p>\n<p>A medium with popularity perpetually in flux, the button has risen and fallen since its creation and notably surged in the 1960s and 1980s as it was used more widely for countercultural movements and protests. Of course, mainstream efforts from political campaigns, public figures, and large-scale events generally still sought out buttons to share their visions. Many of the slogans and broader undertakings of alternative movements that may have evaded popular narratives, however, also are preserved by the object. &#8220;It&#8217;s a people&#8217;s history, too. There are so many things I learned,&#8221; Carter notes. One example involved a series centered on transportation. &#8220;What is this &#8216;good road&#8217; stuff about?&#8221; she wondered. &#8220;I learned that before there was income tax, there was a movement to have infrastructure built.&#8221; Telling a story she didn&#8217;t learn in school, the buttons offered a glimpse into the advocacy of previous decades.</p>\n<p>&#160;</p>\n<p><img loading=\"lazy\" class=\"alignnone size-full wp-image-134736\" src=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-2.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"1200\" height=\"1350\" srcset=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-2.jpg 1200w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-2-640x720.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-2-960x1080.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-2-768x864.jpg 768w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-2-624x702.jpg 624w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 1200px) 100vw, 1200px\" /></p>\n<p>While the manufacturing process and function hasn&#8217;t evolved much, the objects&#8217; value has. Carter notes that when they first emerged, people regarded them as collectibles that were prized as a piece of printed matter. Today, they remain a symbol of the wearer&#8217;s political affiliations and interests.</p>\n<p>Even social media hasn&#8217;t eclipsed the ephemeral object. Although the pithy messages and quips prevalent on sites like Twitter function similarly to the sayings of the button, they lack a material presence and are subject to being deleted or lost when a platform folds. The physical item, on the other hand, has a lasting effect. &#8220;It creates a momento,&#8221; she says. &#8220;It&#8217;s not something you can as easily forget about like a Tweet or something like that because you&#8217;ll come across it in your sock drawer.&#8221; They&#8217;re also a more intentional medium, Carter notes, due to the design, manufacturing, and distribution processes and the effort those require.</p>\n<p>&#160;</p>\n<div id=\"attachment_134738\" style=\"width: 1210px\" class=\"wp-caption alignnone\"><img aria-describedby=\"caption-attachment-134738\" loading=\"lazy\" class=\"wp-image-134738 size-full\" src=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-4.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"1200\" height=\"1350\" srcset=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-4.jpg 1200w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-4-640x720.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-4-960x1080.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-4-768x864.jpg 768w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/carter-4-624x702.jpg 624w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 1200px) 100vw, 1200px\" /><p id=\"caption-attachment-134738\" class=\"wp-caption-text\">From 1981</p></div>\n<p>Overall, buttons are overwhelmingly uplifting, inspiring, or humorous in messaging, even when centered on serious topics or issues.
