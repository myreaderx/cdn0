Chicago-based artist <a href=\"http://chrispappan.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Chris Pappan</a> draws on the tradition of ledger art, a practice that flourished among Native populations throughout the Great Plains from around 1850 to 1920. Rooted in narratives, the renderings depicted the ways of life of Indigenous people and the nuances otherwise left out of mainstream conversations. &#8220;The mid-19th Century was a tumultuous time for the Indigenous peoples of America; the doctrine of Manifest Destiny brought deep pain and suffering but it also introduced new modes of expression,&#8221; says Pappan, who is a citizen of the <a href=\"http://kawnation.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Kaw (Kanza) Nation</a> and of Osage, Lakota and mixed European heritage.</p>\n<p>Using graphite, colored pencils, ink, and water-based media, the artist illustrates black-and-white portraits on a variety of intentionally sourced materials, like municipal ledgers and mining certificates. One artwork (shown below) features five mirrored figures imprinted on Boy Scouts of America neckerchiefs that offer commentary on the destructive practices of the youth organization by recreating appropriated imagery. A similar piece, &#8220;Of White Bread and Miracles,&#8221; evokes the illustrations in the manual <em>Here Is Your Hobby: Indian Dancing and Costumes</em>, which the group often used to teach its members. &#8220;The book is an example of cognitive dissonance as it erases any vestiges of contemporary Native people and homogenizes all Native American cultures while making casual remarks such as &#8216;…get a local Indian to teach you singing and dancing if you can…,'&#8221; Pappan writes.</p>\n<p>&#160;</p>\n<div id=\"attachment_138025\" style=\"width: 2010px\" class=\"wp-caption alignnone\"><img aria-describedby=\"caption-attachment-138025\" loading=\"lazy\" class=\"wp-image-138025 size-full\" src=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/pappan-1.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"2000\" height=\"1028\" srcset=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/pappan-1.jpg 2000w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/pappan-1-640x329.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/pappan-1-960x493.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/pappan-1-1536x790.jpg 1536w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/pappan-1-624x321.jpg 624w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/pappan-1-640x329@2x.jpg 1280w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/pappan-1-960x493@2x.jpg 1920w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 2000px) 100vw, 2000px\" /><p id=\"caption-attachment-138025\" class=\"wp-caption-text\">&#8220;Welcoming the New Dawn&#8221; (2018), mixed media on Evanston municipal ledger, 18 x 36 inches</p></div>\n<p>Despite invoking historical references, the artist imbues his figurative renderings with visions for the future. The lowbrow movement—particularly the melding of technical ability with taboo subject matter—influenced much of his earlier work. More recent projects have honed in on issues of systemic racism and appropriation of sacred objects, which Pappan hopes inspires viewers to question their own complicity. &#8220;I’ve always felt it important to understand boundaries (or rules) so that one can break them and then be able to redefine culture in our own terms. (Native American) Culture is living, and we have the responsibility of its continuity,&#8221; the artist says. He expands on the idea:</p>\n<blockquote><p>Through the medium of indelible ink, I am asserting our identity and our continued existence in the face of attempted erasure and negating the centuries of racist misrepresentations&#8230; In the re-appropriation of an object that may have been considered sacred to some, I hope to impose a sense of what Native people feel when we’re confronted with sacred objects or the bones of our ancestors displayed as macabre entertainment for capitalism.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Pappan is represented by <a href=\"https://www.blueraingallery.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Blue Rain Gallery</a> in Santa Fe. All images © Myriam Dion, shared with permission</p></div>\n<p>For <a href=\"https://www.myriamdion.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Myriam Dion</a>, a newspaper&#8217;s narrative qualities go beyond the text on the page. The Montreal-based artist accentuates the daily briefs and profiles in publications like <em>The Wall Street Journal</em>, <em>Financial Times</em>, and <em>Le Monde</em> by overlaying broadsheets with painstakingly cut newsprint. Brilliantly hued flowers veil an issue focused on the wildfires raging across California, while masked subjects appear in the foreground of a piece about the post-COVID economy. Each tableau centers on one narrative, supporting the journalism with intricate motifs and trimmed photographs spread across the unfolded issue.</p>\n<p>Masking the text-based print with color and woven sections has been a recent addition to Dion&#8217;s practice. &#8220;This operation often doubles or triples the working time, but it helps solidify the works (which are already quite fragile) and gives more depth and possibilities to the patterns that I choose and invent,&#8221; she writes, noting that weaving thin strips through whole editions visually aligns her works more closely with fiber arts.</p>\n<p>More often utilizing vintage copies of North American newspapers than she had <a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/?s=Myriam+Dion\">previously</a>, the artist has identified a through-line in many of the editions. &#8220;For a long time, and even today, the print media has been a forum articulated by and for the male sex, where women have occupied a limited place, and interestingly enough, the newspaper articles I have accumulated document the perception of women in the mass media over the last century,&#8221; she says.</p>\n<p>Dion will be an artist in residence at the <a href=\"https://www.narsfoundation.org/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">NARS Foundation</a> in Brooklyn in 2021, where she plans to create 8-10 new pieces that merge these historic narratives with traditionally feminine art forms, like lacework and embroidery. The idea is subversive and pays &#8220;homage to the female public figures represented in these old newspapers, but more particularly to ordinary women to whom the recognition of any artistic contribution, both from a technical and conceptual point of view, has long been denied by the politics of art.&#8221;</p>\n<p>View more of Dion&#8217;s elaborately collaged projects on <a Wu</a> has visited quiet regions in <a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2019/10/lux-noctis-reuben-wu/\">Bolivia</a>, <a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2016/09/glistening-solar-panels-photographed-by-reuben-wu/\">Nevada&#8217;s SolarReserve</a>, and the rivers of molten sulfur flowing in <a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2015/09/burning-sulphur-rivers-reuben-wu/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Indonesian volcanoes</a> to capture the natural grandeur of the earth&#8217;s outmost layer. In each location, Wu highlights the land&#8217;s beauty by juxtaposing the organic features with artificial light cast by drones flying overhead. The resulting images, of which Wu boasts <a href=\"https://www.behance.net/ReubenWu\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">a rich and diverse collection</a>, employ illuminated geometric shapes to spotlight individual features.</p>\n<p>Wu&#8217;s most recent series, titled <em><a href=\"https://www.behance.net/gallery/107879521/Light-Storm\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Light Storm</a></em>, brought him to the rocky landscapes of Utah and New Mexico—the photographer doesn&#8217;t disclose specifics due to the fragility of the environment. Here, the hovering instruments brighten the stripes and crevices embedded within the stone formations. Like his 2018 series that detailed the melting <a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2018/10/tropical-glacier-photographs-by-reuben-wu/\">Pastoruri Glacier</a> in Peru, <em>Light Storm</em> plays a similar role. &#8220;I felt like it was an attempt to document and preserve the memory of a landscape in peril,&#8221; he shares with Colossal.</p>\n<p>Adamant about leaving no trace on the locales he visits, Wu&#8217;s process allows him to maintain a distance from his rugged subject matter while creating the conditions necessary for such precise shots. He explains:</p>\n<blockquote><p>Instead of the old photographers’ adage of waiting for the right moment, I’m literally creating it from my position behind the camera. It also allows me to have more creative ownership over a photograph of a landscape. Something I’ve been struggling with as a photographer/artist is the idea that a beautiful landscape is doing all the work for me, so this was an opportunity to finally have more artistic control.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Overall, Wu writes, &#8220;the project is about presenting familiar sights in a new and unfamiliar light, renewing your sense of seeing and the experience of Textured swatches of fabric transform stark gallery space into pastel gardens and the idyllic universe of the &#8220;T. A. U. B. I. S.,&#8221; or the bulging-eyed creature with a protruding tongue shown above. Teeming with themes of compassion, joy, and freedom, the sprawling works evoke birth and the warmth of a womb filled with light.</p>\n<p>Part of the collection titled <em>Triumphant Alliance of the Ubiquitous Blossoms of Incarnate Souls</em>—which closed last week at Toronto&#8217;s <a href=\"https://coopercolegallery.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Cooper Cole</a>—Lewis&#8217;s installations imagine an environment centered around abundance, which she explains:</p>\n<blockquote><p>Mutable and devoid of gender, they transmute into blossoms. Every blossom embodies a soul who is alive and listening. T.A.U.B.I.S. blossoms grow year-round, uni-wide, even in most harsh weather and on most hostile planets. The T.A.U.B.I.S communicate and collect intel through these blossoms.</p></blockquote>\n<p>A self-taught artist based in Brooklyn, Lewis hand-dyes vintage curtains, bed sheets, blankets, towels, and clothing that she sews into quilts and looming sculptural figures.
