Their bodies are contorted into cages or squeezed into each other&#8217;s arms, and each looks down or away, a position that makes them appear to lack the power and agency to be free. Cast in dense blocks, the introspective sculptures reflect the artist&#8217;s preference for terracotta and bronze. &#8220;All that we received from the old history is by these two materials,&#8221; he says.</p>\n<p>Most of the pieces shown here are part of the <em>Compressed </em>series, which were born out of the artist&#8217;s own experiences. He tells Colossal:</p>\n<blockquote><p>This project was inspired by my having lived in different places during a short period: detention and compulsory military service in Damascus for four months, then Lebanon for one year and finally arriving to France. Upon arrival in France, at first, I felt liberated from it all. Then I realized that the French live their lives in a complex system that turns them into &#8220;compressed people&#8221; and that we had this in common. This is the first series in which I look at people beyond Syria.</p></blockquote>\n<p>If you&#8217;re in Paris, you can see Khaled DAWWA&#8217;s artwork at numerous spots around the city: his piece titled &#8220;Les Passants&#8221; will be installed in a public spot in Clamart in May 2021, and he&#8217;s also participating in <em>Beautify Paris</em> in June of this year. Currently, he is part of <em>Répare, Reprise</em> at the <a href=\"https://www.citedesartsparis.net/en/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">International City of Arts</a>, a group show that&#8217;s up through July 10, and is in the process of making a film about the artworks on display. id=\"caption-attachment-143420\" class=\"wp-caption-text\">All photos © Henri Calderon for Colossal</p></div>\n<p>Nearly 500 meters of small, red hearts will soon cover an expanse of concrete facing the River Thames in London. Now dubbed the National COVID Memorial Wall, the poignant display publicly commemorates the 150,000 lives lost to the coronavirus pandemic in the United Kingdom so far. Each heart represents one victim, with short messages of grief, love, and remembrance scribed by loved ones in their centers. It takes about ten minutes to walk by the entirety of the project, which serves as a staggering reminder of the virus&#8217;s devastation.</p>\n<p>Coordinated by <a href=\"https://www.crowdfunder.co.uk/user/covid-families-for-justice/profile\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">COVID-19 Bereaved Families For Justice</a>, the two-meter-high wall is situated between the Westminster and Lambeth bridges, opposite the Houses of Parliament. According to <a href=\"https://www.theguardian.com/world/2021/apr/04/wall-of-hearts-grows-as-a-memorial-to-loved-ones-taken-by-coronavirus\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\"><em>The Guardian</em></a>, Matt Fowler helms the ongoing project, which he began a few weeks ago by painting 15,000 hearts on the facade. His father died from the virus last April. &#8220;When you see all the hearts and think what each one represents, it’s absolutely frightening,” Fowler says.</p>\n<p>Organizers still are <a href=\"https://www.crowdfunder.co.uk/fund-the-national-covid-memorial-wall\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">raising money</a> for supplies to complete all 150,000 hearts—although official government statistics currently reflect <a href=\"https://www.theguardian.com/world/2021/apr/04/wall-of-hearts-grows-as-a-memorial-to-loved-ones-taken-by-coronavirus\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">149,000 deaths</a>, which is the largest loss in Europe—that volunteers will continue to paint to account for all victims. Talks are also in the works about preserving the memorial to ensure that it&#8217;s a permanent fixture in London.</p>\n<p>This past weekend, photographer <a href=\"https://www.instagram.com/henricalderon/?igshid=1bh7uyb9scfhl\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Henri Calderon</a> captured images for Colossal that document the memorial&#8217;s progress,
