All images © Hank Willis Thomas, shared with permission</p></div>\n<p>Through his bronze sculptures and public installations, <a href=\"https://www.hankwillisthomas.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Hank Willis Thomas</a> (<a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2018/02/art-in-ad-places-book/\">previously</a>) examines history&#8217;s repetitions. The Brooklyn-based artist critically considers identity, social justice, and pop culture by visually weaving together the remains of the past that surface in present day. &#8220;Art is a platform where histories meet,&#8221; he tells Colossal.</p>\n<p>Thomas&#8217;s sculptural pieces include a series of hands clenching a barbed wire fence, an oversized hair pick lodged into concrete, and a gleaming basketball balancing on players&#8217; fingertips. No matter the medium, the interdisciplinary artist begins by examining advertisements and archival images and the messages those contain. &#8220;The transfer of a photograph into a three-dimensional expression allows the viewer to delve within a photograph and form an intimate understanding of the ideas it represents. That relationship inspires critical thought about the viewer themselves and the world around them,&#8221; he says.</p>\n<p>Many of Thomas&#8217;s artworks reflect on historical moments, like the Holocaust and South African apartheid, and explicitly connect them to contemporary struggles. Photographs of mid-century Germany inspire sculptures, like &#8220;If the Leader Only Knew,&#8221; that evoke images of migrants detained at the United States-Mexico border. He ties a glimpse of mining workers to &#8220;Hands Up, Don&#8217;t Shoot,&#8221; a cry to end state-sanctioned police violence, which informs the outstretched arms in &#8220;Raise Up.&#8221; &#8220;History repeats itself, and art is one cultural framework through which we engage with these profound moments, hopefully awakening our consciousness,&#8221; the Brooklyn-based artist says.</p>\n<p>&#160;</p>\n<div id=\"attachment_136954\" style=\"width: 1410px\" class=\"wp-caption alignnone\"><img aria-describedby=\"caption-attachment-136954\" loading=\"lazy\" class=\"size-full wp-image-136954\" src=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/thomas-9.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"1400\" height=\"1198\" srcset=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/thomas-9.jpg 1400w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/thomas-9-640x548.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/thomas-9-960x821.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/thomas-9-624x534.jpg 624w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/thomas-9-640x548@2x.jpg 1280w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px\" /><p id=\"caption-attachment-136954\" class=\"wp-caption-text\">&#8220;Raise Up&#8221; (2014)</p></div>\n<p>For Thomas, art and activism are inextricable. Art is the human experience. I am also curious about how people and society will change, and I think of my existence within this change as a man, as a Black man,&#8221; he says.</p>\n<p>Thomas&#8217;s work will be part of the group exhibition <em>Barring Freedom</em> at the <a href=\"https://sjmusart.org/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">San José Museum of Art</a>, which runs from October 31, 2020, to April 25, 2021.
