\n<!-- /wp:heading --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>To understand the difference between regular and irregular verbs, it helps to take a closer look at how verbs work in English. They usually follow a pretty simple pattern. I'll illustrate it with the verb \"to walk\".</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>To use \"to walk\" in the present tense, you simply stick a pronoun in front of it: \"I walk\" or \"they walk\". The one exception is the third-person singular form (he/she/it), which has an \"s\" on the end: \"he/she/it walk<em>s</em>\".</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>So far, so simple. Other tenses are just as easy: for the present <em>continuous</em> tense, you stick an \"-ing\" on the end of the verb and combine it with the present tense of the verb \"to be\", as in \"he <em>is</em> walking\". Or you can put an \"-ed\" on the end of the verb to make it past tense: \"I walked\". These aren't the only tenses, of course, but the point is that the different forms of the verb \"to walk\" are made using some simple, consistent patterns that can be applied to many other verbs:</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:list --> \n<ul>\n <li>walk, walks, walked, walking</li>\n <li>help, helps, helped, helping</li>\n <li>play, plays, played, playing</li>\n <li>climb, climbs, climbed, climbing</li>\n</ul> \n<!-- /wp:list --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>And so on.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Most English verbs follow this simple pattern; as such, they're known as <strong>regular</strong> verbs.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>But then, there are verbs like \"to speak\".</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>This word doesn't follow the pattern above; its past-tense version is not \"speaked\" but \"spoke\". Similarly, \"to buy\" becomes \"bought\", not \"buyed\", and \"to throw\" becomes \"threw\", not \"throwed\". These are just a few of the <a href=\"https://languageonschools.com/blog/english-irregular-verbs-list/\">many, many English verbs</a> that don't play by the normal rules. These are the <strong>irregular verbs</strong>.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Spanish is similar. There are some basic patterns that most verbs - the regular verbs - follow, but there are also many irregular exceptions. If you want to communicate effectively in Spanish, you need to learn which verbs are irregular, and what their irregularities are.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>But before we get deeper into the verbs that break the rules, let's review those rules.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:heading --> \n<h2 id=\"aquickrecapofspanishregularverbs\">A Quick Recap of Spanish Regular Verbs</h2> \n<!-- /wp:heading --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Remember that Spanish verbs (regular or irregular) can be divided into three categories, based on the ending of their infinitive form:</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:list --> \n<ul>\n <li>\"<em>-ar</em>\" verbs, such as <em>hablar</em> (to speak), <em>cantar</em> (to sing), and <em>bailar</em> (to dance)</li>\n <li>\"<em>-er</em>\" verbs, such as <em>deber</em> (to owe), <em>correr</em> (to run), and <em>comprender</em> (to understand)</li>\n <li>\"<em>-ir</em>\" verbs, such as <em>vivir</em> (to live), <em>existir</em> (to exist), and <em>ocurrir</em> (\"to happen\")</li>\n</ul> \n<!-- /wp:list --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>The regular present tense forms in each case are:</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:html --> \n<div style=\"overflow-x: auto; This might look like a <em>o</em> to <em>ue</em> stem-changing verb, but <em>when the stem changes, you must add an \"h\" to the beginning</em>:</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:list --> \n<ul>\n <li><em>huelo</em> - I smell</li>\n <li><em>hueles</em> - you smell</li>\n <li><em>huele</em> - he/she/it smells</li>\n <li><em>olemos</em> - we smell</li>\n <li><em>oléis</em> - you (pl.) smell</li>\n <li><em>huelen</em> - they smell</li>\n</ul> \n<!-- /wp:list --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>(Remember that an \"h\" in Spanish is always silent, so this extra letter doesn't have any effect on the pronunciation.)</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Second, the verb <em>jugar</em>. It’s the only example of a verb whose stem changes from a <em>u</em> to a <em>ue</em>:</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:list --> \n<ul>\n <li><em>juego</em> - I play</li>\n <li><em>juegas</em> - you (s.( play</li>\n <li><em>juega</em> - he/she/it plays</li>\n <li><em>jugamos</em> - we play</li>\n <li><em>jugáis</em> - you (pl.) play</li>\n <li><em>juegan</em> - they play</li>\n</ul> \n<!-- /wp:list --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Third, two verbs exist that change an \"i\" to an \"ie\". They are <em>adquirir</em> (to acquire) and <em>inquirir</em> (to inquire). So in the first-person singular they're <em>adquiero</em> and <em>inquiero</em>, respectively. Can you figure out the other five present-tense forms of <em>adquirir</em> and <em>inquirir</em>? Hopefully by now it should be easy.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:heading --> \n<h2 id=\"spanishverbswithanirregularyoform\">Spanish Verbs With an Irregular \"yo\" Form</h2> \n<!-- /wp:heading --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>I told you earlier that <em>decir</em> (\"to say\") is an <em>e-</em> to <em>-i</em> stem-changing verb. But it’s not only that. <em>Decir</em> is also one of a small number of verbs which has a non-standard <em>yo</em> form.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Remember that <em>yo</em> means \"I\". \"I say\" is <em>(yo) digo</em>, which isn't what you'd expect if you followed the rules above.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>To be clear, here are all six present-tense forms of <em>decir</em>:</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:list --> \n<ul>\n <li><em>digo</em> - I say</li>\n <li><em>dices</em> - you (s.) say</li>\n <li><em>dice</em> - he/she says</li>\n <li><em>decimos</em> - we say</li>\n <li><em>decís</em> - you (pl.) say</li>\n <li><em>dicen</em> - they say</li>\n</ul> \n<!-- /wp:list --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>As you can see, the first form uses the weird <em>-go</em> suffix. The rest of the forms proceed as normal, subject to the stem changes that I already explained.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Several other common Spanish verbs follow this pattern in the present tense. The first-person singular form is irregular; all other forms are either regular or, as in the case of <em>decir</em>, have a stem change.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Here's what you need to learn. For each verb, I'll give the infinitive, the first-person singular (which is irregular), and the second-person singular (so you can see the stem change, or lack of it).</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:heading {\"level\":3} --> \n<h3 id=\"irregularyoformwithnostemchange\">Irregular \"yo\" form with no stem change</h3> \n<!-- /wp:heading --> \n<!-- wp:list --> \n<ul>\n <li><em>conocer</em> - \"to know\" - <em>yo <strong>conozco</strong></em>, <em>tú conoces</em></li>\n <li><em>dar</em> - \"to give\" - <em>yo <strong>doy</strong></em>, <em>tú das</em></li>\n <li><em>hacer</em> - \"to do, make\" - <em>yo <strong>hago</strong></em>, <em>tú haces</em></li>\n <li><em>poner</em> - \"to put\" - <em>yo <strong>pongo</strong></em>, <em>tú pones</em></li>\n <li><em>salir</em> - \"to exit\" - <em>yo <strong>salgo</strong></em>, <em>tú sales</em></li>\n <li><em>traer</em> - \"to bring\" - <em>yo <strong>traigo</strong></em>, <em>tú traes</em></li>\n <li><em>ver</em> - \"to see\" - <em>yo <strong>veo</strong></em>, <em>tú ves</em></li>\n <li><em>oír</em> - \"to hear\" - <em>yo <strong>oigo</strong></em>, <em>tú oyes</em></li>\n <li><em>saber</em> - \"to know\" - <em>yo <strong>sé</strong></em>, <em>tú sabes</em></li>\n <li><em>ir</em> - \"to go\" - <em>yo <strong>voy</strong></em>, <em>tú vas</em></li>\n <li><em>estar</em> - \"to be\" - <em>yo <strong>estoy</strong></em>, <em>tú estás</em></li>\n <li><em>caber</em> - \"to fit\" - <em>yo <strong>quepo</strong></em>, <em>tú cabes</em></li>\n <li><em>lucir</em> - \"to wear\" - <em>yo <strong>luzco</strong></em>, <em>tú luces</em></li>\n <li><em>valer</em> - \"to be worth\" - <em>yo <strong>valgo</strong></em>, <em>tú vales</em></li>\n</ul> \n<!-- /wp:list --> \n<!-- wp:heading {\"level\":3} --> \n<h3 id=\"irregularyoformwithastemchange\">Irregular \"yo\" form with a stem change</h3> \n<!-- /wp:heading --> \n<!-- wp:list --> \n<ul>\n <li><em>decir</em> - \"to say\" - <em>yo <strong>digo</strong></em>, <em>tú dices</em></li>\n <li><em>tener</em> - \"to have\" - <em>yo <strong>tengo</strong></em>, <em>tú tienes</em></li>\n <li><em>venir</em> - \"to come\" - <em>yo <strong>vengo</strong></em>, <em>tú vienes</em></li>\n</ul> \n<!-- /wp:list --> \n<!-- wp:heading --> \n<h2 id=\"ser\">Ser</h2> \n<!-- /wp:heading --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>It's time to look at the biggest and baddest of all Spanish irregular verbs: <em>ser</em>, which means \"to be\".</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Like its English counterpart, <em>ser</em> is highly irregular - and not just in the first-person singular. Here are the six present-tense forms of <em>ser</em>:</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:list --> \n<ul>\n <li><em>soy</em> - I am</li>\n <li><em>eres</em> - you (s.) are</li>\n <li><em>es</em> - he/she/it is</li>\n <li><em>somos</em> - we are</li>\n <li><em>sóis</em> - you (pl.) are</li>\n <li><em>son</em> - they are</li>\n</ul> \n<!-- /wp:list --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>I recommend you learn these conjugations by memory as soon as possible. It's probably the most important irregular verb in Spanish, and it will show up in most of the sentences you’ll see, hear, read, or speak.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:heading --> \n<h2 id=\"haber\">Haber</h2> \n<!-- /wp:heading --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Another highly irregular (and important) verb is <em>haber</em>. The dictionary might tell you that <em>haber</em> means \"to have\", but this doesn't paint the full picture.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>To say \"I have a dog\" in Spanish, you'd say <em>\"<strong>tengo</strong> un perro\"</em>. <em>Tengo</em>, as we saw above, is the irregular first-person singular form of <em>tener</em>, and <em>tener</em> is the normal way to say \"have\" in this sense in Spanish.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>So where does <em>haber</em> come in?</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Think of an English sentence like \"I <strong>have</strong> eaten \". The word \"have\" is doing something different here. It doesn't mean ownership or possession, which is what <em>tener</em> is for.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Instead it's a grammatical device that changes the tense of the word. In this case, it tells you that the action took place in the past.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>This is the primary function of <em>haber</em> in Spanish: it's used in compound tenses, like the \"have\" in \"I have eaten\".</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>Here's how it's conjugated (and I'll stick with \"eaten\", <em>comido</em>, as my example):</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:list --> \n<ul>\n <li><em>he comido</em> - I have eaten</li>\n <li><em>has comido</em> - you (s.) have eaten</li>\n <li><em>ha comido</em> - he/she/it has eaten</li>\n <li><em>hemos comido</em> - we have eaten</li>\n <li><em>habéis comido</em> - you (pl.) have eaten</li>\n <li><em>han comido</em> - they have eaten</li>\n</ul> \n<!-- /wp:list --> \n<!-- wp:heading --> \n<h2 id=\"theendofthebeginning\">The End of the Beginning</h2> \n<!-- /wp:heading --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>This might seem like a lot, but you don’t need to learn everything now.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>For one thing, if you still haven’t got a solid grasp of <em>regular</em> verb endings, you should work on that before worrying too much about irregular endings. If you need help with these, take a look at <a href=\"https://www.fluentin3months.com/spanish-verb-conjugation/\">Benny’s post on the three main Spanish tenses</a>.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph --> \n<p>When you feel ready, come back to these irregular endings, and, as always, <strong><a href=\"https://www.fluentin3months.com/embarrassing-mistakes/\">don’t be afraid to make mistakes</a></strong>! If you forget which verbs are irregular, and say something like <em>yo sabo</em> instead of <em>yo sé</em>, people will still understand what you mean.</p> \n<!-- /wp:paragraph --> \n<!-- wp:paragraph
