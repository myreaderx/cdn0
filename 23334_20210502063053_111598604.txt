And do you know what would have made this burrito considerably better? Avocado ranch sauce and/or possibly more beef!</p>\n<p>As presented, the burrito came up short in terms of taste. While the red tortilla strips remained crunchy, which was great for texture, they tasted indiscernible from the regular burrito shell. The seasoned beef was pretty skimpy (though, to be fair, this item was $1) and, you know, Taco Bell beef. The lettuce was warm and didn’t add anything, nor did the standard cheddar cheese. The sour cream was good, but it didn’t keep the burrito from being mostly dry.</p>\n<p>Look, if you’ve eaten at Taco Bell, you’ve had this burrito in some form or another.</p>\n<p><img loading=\"lazy\" style=\"display:block; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;\" src=\"https://i2.wp.com/www.theimpulsivebuy.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/tbprimo4.jpeg?resize=600%2C600&#038;ssl=1\" alt=\"Taco Bell Primo Burritos Enchilada\" title=\"tbprimo4.jpeg\" border=\"0\" width=\"600\" height=\"600\" data-recalc-dims=\"1\" /></p>\n<p>This brings us to the Chicken Enchilada Primo Burrito. This new offering features white meat chicken strips, reduced fat sour cream, seasoned rice, enchilada sauce, and cheddar. And if you think THAT ingredient list sounds familiar, well… okay, so I don’t know that Taco Bell did this <em>exact</em> burrito before, but all the way back in olden times — from 2004-2007, then briefly resurrected in 2010 — it did a Chicken Enchilada Grilled Stuft Burrito. That was the same thing, except it had the patented three cheese blend instead of plain cheddar, and the whole thing was grilled. And you know what would have made this better? A three cheese blend and grilling!</p>\n<p>If this all sounds like I’m simply trying to be an anti-Bell contrarian, I apologize; the fact of the matter is, this burrito, like its newborn sibling, was a boring rehash, something that, let’s face it, Taco Bell is prone to doing.</p>\n<p>While the Enchilada burrito won’t be winning any awards, it was definitely better than the Taco version. The enchilada sauce — milder than any you’ve had before, but still <em>decent</em> — was mixed with the sour cream to ensure that dryness wasn’t a factor, and the seasoned rice kept everything from being too gloopy of a mess. Even still, this isn’t something I can imagine spending money on again.</p>\n<p>In the end, neither one of these did much for me, nor can I imagine them sticking around for long. (If, in fact, they even ever get a national rollout.)
