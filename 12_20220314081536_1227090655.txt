Beyond comfort, it’s reflection, as well as memory. Every time they see or touch that quilt, they can remember the good times.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Ultimately, SJSA empowers youth to use their voices and requires tactile processing of issues that often seem bigger than all of us. The works represent the Buenos Aires-based artist’s fascination with the cycle of time and how flowers convey youth but also the “unbearable finitude of life,&#8221; she tells Colossal. “With their incredible beauty and freshness, although transitory, they fill us with hope and convince us that life is worth living.&#8221;</p>\n<p>Morelli works as if using a macro lens, and sunlight, in particular, informs her practice. For each piece, she studies its effect on the fresh flower’s textures and colors throughout the day—“Sometimes objects are only an excuse to paint the light,” she says—and photographs the lively blooms, giving her a record to work off of as they wilt.</p>\n<p>Often taking a month or two to finish a single work, the artist&#8217;s process is puzzle-like. She first examines the blend of pigments and saturation in small, abstract pieces and then places the information back together through painstaking layering and precise brushstrokes. It&#8217;s the paint&#8217;s second or third application in which she sees her botanics “come to life,&#8221; she explains. “I don’t work with more than three or four layers because I believe the painting must keep some freshness too, like the flowers I’m painting. That’s why I try to apply the right color and value I see on the model from the very start.”</p>\n<p>Having started painting as a child, Morelli says that formative experience taught her to think about art as its own language. “Images can say much more than words, and even faster, we know that already, and through a painting, you can show many feelings in a single message,” she explains.</p>\n<p>Morelli will exhibit her works at <a href=\"https://www.arts.org.tw/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Art Revolution Taipei</a> in May 2022, and you can follow her practice on <a href=\"https://www.instagram.com/mariamarta_morelli/?hl=en\">Instagram</a>. spigot.</p>\n<p>Although it shows a minuscule portion of the waste produced worldwide, the resulting installation, titled &#8220;<a href=\"https://turnofftheplastictap.com/\" target=\"_blank\" href=\"https://www.alextennapel.nl/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Alex ten Napel</a>. No matter the situation, the red-faced birds are wholly themselves, lurching from one spot to the next, burying themselves within masses of feathers, and spreading their wings as if they&#8217;ll finally lift off the ground despite being notoriously poor fliers. Chickens are known for their awkward gaits and distinct attitudes and are also the latest subjects of ten Napel&#8217;s portraiture.</p>\n<p>Having focused his lens on people for about 25 years, the Amsterdam-based photographer realized that documenting the fowl occupying his henhouse would be a compelling challenge. This interest culminated in his ongoing <em>Hens and Roosters</em> series, which shines studio lighting on the oddly photogenic creatures and captures their unique mannerisms as they strut around the space. &#8220;Photographing became unexpected, exciting, and out of control. I was impressed by these little feathered creatures who had the power to tell me to be patient and to wait for the right moment,&#8221; ten Napel shares.</p>\n<p>Prints of the moody birds are available on <a href=\"https://www.alextennapel.nl/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">his site</a>, and keep an eye on <a href=\"https://www.instagram.com/alextennapel/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">his Instagram</a> for news about a <em>Hens and Roosters</em> book.
