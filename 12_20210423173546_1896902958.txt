All images © Elena Zaycman, shared with permission</p></div>\n<p>From her studio in St. Petersburg, artist <a href=\"http://elenazaycman.com/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Elena Zaycman</a> creates delicate flowers and tropical plants from vibrant stained glass. She strays from the traditional two-dimensional panels to produce lifelike forms that resemble fleshy petals and curved leaves found in nature. Whether a pair of tulips or fanned palm, Zaycman&#8217;s translucent designs refract light and cast tinted shadows in an array of organic shapes around the space.</p>\n<p>Having worked with the medium for nearly a decade, the artist tells Colossal that prior to creating the smaller sculptures she collaborated with her sister on expansive projects that required a lengthy, complex installation in homes and other spaces. She began to produce the mounted pieces as a way to circumvent that process and make the art form more accessible to those without the resources for large, permanent works. Today, her sculptures often reflect vegetation and natural life spotted during travel—an encounter with a stray puppy on <a href=\"http://elenazaycman.com/bali-line\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">a trip to Bali</a> informed many of the pieces shown here—or evoke playful, geometric characters, like in her <a href=\"http://elenazaycman.com/monstrics\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\"><em>Monstrics</em></a> collection.</p>\n<p>Zaycman was recently featured in the second edition of <a href=\"https://www.atelierardent.com/store/p/we-are-makers-edition-two\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\"><em>We Are Makers</em></a>, and she sells downloadable patterns for a variety of moths and insects on <a href=\"https://www.etsy.com/shop/elenazaycman\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Etsy</a>. Ranging from life-sized figurative sculptures with an eccentric twist to sprawling landscapes mimicking dense metropolises, Nimako&#8217;s artworks are rooted in the visionary realm of <a href=\"https://agakhanmuseum.org/exhibitions/ekow-nimako-building-black-civilizations\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Afrofuturism</a>, which &#8220;explores the intersection of technology and race to visualize a powerful future for the African diaspora&#8221; through a hearty dose of hope and strength.</p>\n<p>His ongoing series, <a href=\"https://ekownimako.com/artwork\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\"><em>Building Black</em></a>, is an expansive collection that encompasses fantastical masks inspired by West African tradition and mythological characters that draw on folklore and proverbs. Another facet includes a broad, architectural sculpture that expands 30-square-feet. The 2019 work is titled &#8220;Kumbi Saleh 3020 CE,&#8221; a reference to the capital city of the ancient <a href=\"https://www.ancient.eu/Ghana_Empire/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noreferrer noopener\">Ghana Empire</a> that&#8217;s thought to have contained a mosque, a central square, and various circuit walls.</p>\n<p>&#160;</p>\n<div id=\"attachment_143821\" style=\"width: 2010px\" class=\"wp-caption alignnone\"><img aria-describedby=\"caption-attachment-143821\" loading=\"lazy\" class=\"wp-image-143821 size-full\" src=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-1.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"2000\" height=\"1851\" srcset=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-1.jpg 2000w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-1-640x592.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-1-960x888.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-1-1536x1422.jpg 1536w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-1-624x578.jpg 624w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-1-640x592@2x.jpg 1280w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-1-960x888@2x.jpg 1920w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 2000px) 100vw, 2000px\" /><p id=\"caption-attachment-143821\" class=\"wp-caption-text\">Left: &#8220;Simis&#8221; (2019). Right: &#8220;Esun&#8221; (2020)</p></div>\n<p>Running through each of these artworks is a fluid understanding of time and space that blurs the distinction between generations, locations, and histories in order to imagine a new reality. &#8220;We are all living proof of our ancestors, all their joy, love, knowledge, and pain. They live in our DNA,&#8221; the Ghanaian-Canadian artist says. &#8220;Aesthetically, I enjoy taking elements from bygone eras and creating futuristic landscapes, particularly of African utopias to imagine a liberated existence for us all.&#8221;</p>\n<p>That blurred temporality that foregrounds his sculptures and installations parallels his own trajectory, as well. &#8220;My art practice developed when I was four years old, as I constantly told myself I want to do this (play with LEGO) forever, and sometimes it feels as though my future self communicated with my past self, astrally perhaps, to ensure this very specific destiny manifested,&#8221; he says, noting that the plastic blocks have remained a fixture in both his personal and professional life since becoming a father.</p>\n<p>&#160;</p>\n<div id=\"attachment_143822\" style=\"width: 2010px\" class=\"wp-caption alignnone\"><img aria-describedby=\"caption-attachment-143822\" loading=\"lazy\" class=\"wp-image-143822 size-full\" src=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-2.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"2000\" height=\"809\" srcset=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-2.jpg 2000w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-2-640x259.jpg 640w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-2-960x388.jpg 960w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-2-1536x621.jpg 1536w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-2-624x252.jpg 624w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-2-640x259@2x.jpg 1280w, https://www.thisiscolossal.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/nimako-2-960x388@2x.jpg 1920w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 2000px) 100vw, 2000px\" /><p id=\"caption-attachment-143822\" class=\"wp-caption-text\">&#8220;Kumbi Saleh 3020 CE&#8221; he shares. &#8220;The form and content, the embodiment of life, always comes first with my work.&#8221;</p>\n<p>In 2017, Nimako published a guide to LEGO animals, <a href=\"https://bookshop.org/a/96/9781631592997\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\"><em>Beasts from Bricks</em></a>, and plans to continue teaching with a tutorial for building afrofuturistic worlds that&#8217;ll launch on <a href=\"http://www.ekownimako.com\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">his site</a> this June. href=\"http://kanakoa.be/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Kanako Abe</a> (<a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2018/04/stylized-paper-cut-artworks-by-kanako-abe/\">previously</a>) carves exquisite silhouettes of children and young adults who are awash in seas of fish or occupied by quiet campouts. She utilizes the traditional Japanese art form called Kirie—which translates to cut picture—a technique that Abe begins with a sketch before slicing the delicate material with a variety of knives. &#8220;I don’t have a chance to change the design once I start cutting, so I find it challenging,&#8221; the Seattle-based artist says. &#8220;I have to think of the right patterns, controlling negative space, and make sure all the lines are connected so the art won’t fall apart once it’s finished.&#8221; A single piece can take anywhere from six to 60 hours to complete.</p>\n<p>Abe shifted to full-time in 2020 and now balances her practice between commissions and ongoing personal projects, a few of which she&#8217;ll be sharing soon on <a href=\"https://www.instagram.com/abemanatee/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Instagram</a>. No matter the context, each artwork reflects a broader connection to nature and its ability to provide an escape from the complications and heartbreak of the current moment. &#8220;I find the process of art-making is a way for me to meditate on everyday thoughts and emotions, and it’s much easier for me to express complex feelings or emotions visually than verbally,&#8221; she tells Colossal. &#8220;The cycle of nature teaches us about the power of letting go or accept things as they are and that there’s a silver lining in everything.&#8221;</p>\n<p>If you&#8217;re in San Francisco, you can see Abe&#8217;s intricate portraits at her September solo show at <a href=\"https://raredevice.net/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Rare Device</a>. The new exhibition, simply titled &#8220;Life,&#8221; is the work of acclaimed Danish-Iceland artist <a href=\"https://www.olafureliasson.net/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Olafur Eliasson</a> (<a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/?s=Olfaur+Eliasson\">previously</a>), who set the Swiss institution awash in floating ferns, dwarf water lilies, shell flowers, red root floaters, and water caltrops.</p>\n<p>To install the sprawling project, Eliasson removed the windows on one side of the museum&#8217;s facade, which allows visitors and nearby wildlife to enter the space at any time of day or night. The open-air environment subjects the manufactured reservoir indoors to the naturally occurring elements outside the building, like the weather, daylight, humidity, and smells and sounds of nearby public gardens. At night, a combination of UV lights and a fluorescent dye called uranine radiate brilliant colors throughout the water.</p>\n<p><a href=\"https://olafureliasson.net/life/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">A prismatic livestream</a>—Eliasson outfitted some of the cameras with apparatuses that mimic the sensory experiences of animals and insects—captures how the immersive space changes with each moment, especially as the surface reflects shadows and passersby. These interactions between human and non-human species foreground the project, which was inspired by anthropologist <a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natasha_Myers\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Natasha Myers</a> who&#8217;s advocated for the advent of the &#8220;planthroposcene.&#8221; An alternative to the anthropocene, Myers&#8217; concept is &#8220;rooted in the knowledge that plants are what made this planet liveable,&#8221; <a href=\"https://life.fondationbeyeler.ch/en/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">a statement</a> says, clarifying that although the gallery is overrun with water, Eliasson&#8217;s goal is to evidence the interconnectivity inherent in nature.</p>\n<p>Fondation Beyeler is housing Eliasson&#8217;s &#8220;Life&#8221; through July. Find more of the artist&#8217;s monumental projects on <a href=\"https://olafureliasson.net/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">his studio’s site</a> and <a href=\"https://www.instagram.com/studioolafureliasson/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Instagram</a>. From a woman resting on a bicycle to two surfers readying for the water, the oil-based paintings observe moments of respite and pleasure at a monumental scale, sometimes spanning nearly nine feet across.</p>\n<p>Although she surrounds her subjects with vivid patches of color and portrays them wearing bright garments, Sherald (<a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/?s=Amy+Sherald\">previously</a>) continues to render her subjects&#8217; skin in her signature grayscale, which she&#8217;s <a href=\"https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2020/06/amy-sherald-grayscale-portraits/\">described in recent years</a> as a way to have the figures read &#8220;in a universal way, where they could become a part of the mainstream art historical narrative.&#8221; This new series also features elements synonymous with American culture, including a white picket fence, Barbie T-shirt, and retro convertible.</p>\n<p>The collection&#8217;s title draws on the work of <a href=\"https://www.britannica.com/biography/Anna-Julia-Cooper\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Anna Julia Cooper</a>, an educator who in 1892 wrote <em>A Voice From the South by a Black Woman of the South</em>. In the classic Black feminist text, Cooper described Black people as &#8220;&#8216;the great American fact’; the one objective reality on which scholars sharpened their wits, and at which orators and statesmen fired their eloquence.&#8221; This understanding structures Sherald&#8217;s work and provides guideposts for considering &#8220;public Blackness,&#8221; particularly as the Georgia-born artist portrays figures with rich personal lives full of ease, relaxation, and joy. When much of Black life historically has centered around grappling with injustice and social issues, Sherald&#8217;s turn inward provides a nuanced view of her subjects.</p>\n<p>The five paintings
