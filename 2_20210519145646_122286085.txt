Physicists have now discovered a rare material known as a quasicrystal in one of the trinitite samples. According to <a href=\"https://www.pnas.org/cgi/doi/10.1073/pnas.2101350118\">a new paper</a> published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, that makes the discovery the oldest <a href=\"https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/anthropogenic\">anthropogenic</a> quasicrystal yet known.</p>\n<p>The <a href=\"https://nautil.us/blog/the-unlikely-rocks-found-in-mosques-siberia--outer-space\">very definition of a crystal</a> assumes a precisely symmetrical ordering of atoms in periodic patterns that repeat over and over in a 3D lattice. The patterns look the same no matter which direction you look at them, but quasicrystals are different. They clearly follow mathematical rules, but each cell has a slightly different configuration of cells nearby rather than repeating in an identical pattern. It's that unique structure that gives quasicrystals their unusual properties.</p>\n<p>Think about tiling a bathroom floor. The tiles can only be in certain symmetrical shapes (triangles, squares, or hexagons); otherwise, you wouldn't be able to fit them together without leaving gaps or overlapping tiles. Pentagons, icosahedrons, and similar shapes with different symmetries that never precisely repeat just won’t work—except in the case of quasicrystals, where nature decided they <em>could</em> work.
